Welcome

Let's learn to read media like a skeptic.

Every day you scroll past headlines and posts engineered to grab your attention before your brain has a chance to think. This isn't a quiz to catch you out — it's a hands-on lab. You'll look at real-feeling (but fictional) examples, form your own gut reaction, then reveal what's really going on underneath.

Why this matters: Misleading headlines and emotionally engineered posts spread faster than calm, accurate ones — because outrage and fear are more "clickable" than nuance. Recognizing the pattern is the fastest way to stop being an easy target.

Challenge 1

Headline Detective

Headlines are written to earn a click in under two seconds — sometimes by stretching, cherry-picking, or flat-out overstating what the article actually says. This gap between headline and content is called a headline–content mismatch, and it's one of the most common forms of everyday misinformation.

Everyday example: A headline says "Study PROVES coffee cures anxiety" — the actual study found a small, preliminary correlation in 20 people. The headline isn't lying outright, but it's exaggerating certainty the data doesn't support.

Emotion Detector

Beyond headlines, social posts and captions are often built around one goal: make you feel something strong enough to stop scrolling, react, or share — before you think it through. Fear, outrage, and awe are the three biggest engagement drivers online.

Everyday example: A caption reading "They don't want you to know this one trick" isn't sharing information as much as it's manufacturing curiosity and a sense of a hidden enemy — a classic engagement hook.

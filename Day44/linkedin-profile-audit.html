Headline

3 / 10
"Another student headline. Next."
"Aspiring Java Developer | Java • OOPs • MySQL • DSA (Learning) | AI Enthusiast"
Problem
"Aspiring" and "DSA (Learning)" put your gaps in the headline itself — the one field that shows up next to your name everywhere. "AI Enthusiast" is filler; it's not a skill, it's a mood.
Why it hurts
Recruiters scan headlines in search results before ever opening your profile. "Learning" and "Aspiring" read as "not ready" — which is the fastest way to get filtered out before a human even sees Eventora.
Invisible cost
Recruiters search "Java Developer intern" or "Spring Boot" — not "AI Enthusiast." Your headline currently doesn't match the queries that would actually surface you.

About — First 2 Lines

3 / 10
"Sounds like every third-year CS student. Scrolls past."
"I am a B.Tech Computer Science 3rd year student with a strong interest in Java and full-stack web development."
Problem
This opens with your bio, not a hook. "Strong interest" is passive — it tells them how you feel, not what you can do.
Why it hurts
Only ~2 lines show before "see more." If those lines don't earn a click, the rest of your About — including your actual project — is never read.
Invisible cost
Eventora, your strongest proof point, is buried past the fold. Right now it might as well not exist to a scrolling recruiter.

About — Full Section

4 / 10
"Fine. Forgettable."
"...actively seeking internship opportunities to learn, grow, and contribute."
Problem
"Learn, grow, and contribute" is one of the most repeated closing lines on student profiles — recruiters have read it thousands of times. No numbers, no named project, no call to action.
Why it hurts
Generic closers signal a copy-paste profile, even when the person behind it isn't generic at all — which, based on Eventora, you're not.
Invisible cost
Without a CTA, someone who reads the whole thing and likes it still doesn't know what to do next — message you? Visit a link? You're leaving interest on the table.

DAY 4 Comment on 5 posts in your target space

Use the Value Comment formula on posts from Java/full-stack developers or recruiters:

Agree+ Add insight+ Ask a question

Example: "Agree that DSA feels disconnected from real projects at first — building Eventora actually made recursion and query optimization click for me because I hit real bottlenecks. Curious how you balance DSA practice with shipping projects during internships?"

DAY 5 Publish Post #2 — lessons-learned / hot take
Unpopular opinion: DSA practice > tutorial hell. I spent months watching "build a project in 1 hour" videos. My portfolio grew. My problem-solving didn't. The shift that actually helped: instead of learning a new framework every week, I sat with hard DSA problems until they clicked — even when one problem took 3 hours. Now when I build something (like Eventora, my last full-stack project), I'm not just copying patterns — I'm actually reasoning through the logic. If you're a CS student stuck in tutorial hell: pick ONE hard problem today. Sit with it longer than feels comfortable. What's helped you get unstuck? Genuinely curious 👇
~800 characters — under the 1,300 limit
DAY 6 Engage + send 5 more connection requests
  • Reply to every comment on both posts — a reply within a few hours doubles a post's reach
  • Send 5 more requests using the same template, targeting people who engaged with your posts
DAY 7 Review profile views & connection acceptance rate
  • Profile views up: your headline and keywords are working — you're showing up in more searches.
  • Profile views flat: revisit your headline keywords, and make sure your posts are public, not connections-only.
  • High acceptance rate (>40%): your connection message is resonating — keep the template.
  • Low acceptance rate: you may be targeting too senior/cold an audience — try alumni or people who engaged with your posts first.
